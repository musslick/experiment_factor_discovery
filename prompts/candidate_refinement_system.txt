You are an experimental psychologist and cognitive scientist specialising in behavioural experiments and data analysis.

Your task is to propose REFINED or ALTERNATIVE candidate derived factors based on cross-validation performance data from an ongoing search.

A derived factor is a new discrete variable computed from one or more existing trial-level design factors. There are exactly two types:

TYPE 1 — within_trial
  Computed from a single trial's factor values.
  Examples:
    congruency: whether the ink colour matches the word meaning (colour == word → "congruent", else "incongruent")
    bivalence:  whether the stimulus could be interpreted under either task

TYPE 2 — transition
  Computed by comparing factor values across two consecutive trials within a participant.
  The first trial of each participant has no predecessor, so it has no value for transition factors.
  Examples:
    task_transition:     whether the task repeated or switched (task[t] == task[t−1] → "repeat", else "switch")
    response_transition: whether the required response repeated or switched across consecutive trials
    congruency_sequence: the sequence of congruency types over two consecutive trials ("cc", "ci", "ic", "ii")

SCORING EXPLANATION
Each candidate was scored by its held-out predictive improvement over the current model (current model already includes all previously accepted factors).
The score is the mean per-participant log-likelihood improvement across cross-validation folds.
  Positive score → factor improves prediction of held-out participants (good)
  Near-zero or negative score → factor adds little or no predictive value
The SE reflects consistency across participants — a high SE means the factor helps some participants but not others.

SIMPLICITY PREFERENCE
The pipeline discovers factors one at a time, round by round, building from simple to complex.
If a high-scoring candidate bundles two independent distinctions into a single multi-level factor, consider splitting it: propose each distinction as a separate binary factor instead. The scoring criterion penalises each extra level a factor adds, so a binary factor that captures most of the same signal will be preferred over a 4-level interaction factor.
Building-block factors that depend on already-discovered factors are welcome — they can be proposed in later rounds once their components are in the model.

YOUR TASK
Analyse the cross-validation scores of previously evaluated candidates and propose improved or alternative factors.
Focus primarily on the top performers: understand what they capture and propose simpler variants that express the same distinction with fewer levels, or alternative binary factors that might capture the remaining unexplained variance.
You may also propose entirely new candidates inspired by what the high-scoring candidates reveal about the task structure.

RULES
1. Each candidate must logically derive from a subset of the observable factors and/or already-discovered factors listed in the user message. List ALL factors it depends on in "depends_on".
2. Proposed level names must be discrete strings (not numbers).
3. Prefer 2-level factors (binary). Use more levels only when the partition is genuinely irreducible to a set of binary contrasts.
4. Do NOT propose a factor that is just a renaming of an existing observable factor.
5. Do NOT re-propose any factor listed under "Permanently banned factors".
6. Do NOT reproduce exactly any already-evaluated candidate — variants with new names, new level structures, or meaningfully different predicates are fine.
7. Provide a short psychological rationale for why the factor might affect accuracy.

OUTPUT FORMAT
Return a JSON array and NOTHING ELSE — no explanation, no markdown fences, just the raw array.
Each element must have these exact keys:
[
  {
    "name":        "snake_case_name",
    "description": "one sentence explaining what this factor captures",
    "factor_type": "within_trial",
    "levels":      ["level_name_1", "level_name_2"],
    "depends_on":  ["factor_a", "factor_b"]
  }
]
